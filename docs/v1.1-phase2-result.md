# Landfall v1.1, Phase 2 result — hazard-layer investigation for Rolly (Goni 2020)

## Why this phase exists

Phase 1's RMSF recalibration closed Haiyan's gap (18.6× → 1.18× under) but left Rolly
at 29.6× under and flipped Odette to 3.9–7.7× over. Working hypothesis: Rolly's
error is partly hazard-side — a compact, rapidly-weakening storm whose true peak the
3-hourly best track and parametric wind field might smear away.

## How ingestion actually works (task 1)

`landfall/hazard/tracks.py::get_track` calls `TCTracks.from_ibtracs_netcdf(storm_id=...)`
at CLIMADA defaults: `provider=None` (resolves to CLIMADA's per-variable agency-fallback
chain `['official_3h', 'usa', 'tokyo', ...]`) and `rescale_windspeeds=True` (every
agency's native wind convention already linearly rescaled to 1-minute-sustained
equivalent, via Knapp & Kruk (2010)'s published per-agency factors — this is CLIMADA's
own behavior, not something Landfall does separately).

Checked Rolly's actual ingested track directly: `provider` attribute reads
`ibtracs_mixed:lat(official_3h),lon(official_3h),wind(official_3h),pres(official_3h),
rmw(usa),poci(usa),roci(usa)` — position/wind/pressure come from JMA (WP basin's
official RSMC, 3-hourly), RMW/POCI/ROCI fall back to USA/JTWC because **JMA does not
report a radius-of-maximum-wind variable in IBTrACS at all**, for any storm, not just
this one. Nothing in `landfall/hazard/` calls `.equal_timestep()`, and
`TropCyclone.from_tracks()` doesn't resample time itself — it evaluates the Holland
field at exactly the track's own reported timestamps (61 discrete 3-hourly snapshots
for Rolly) and takes the per-centroid max over the whole track. Grid: 150 arcsec
(≈4.6 km) centroids (`landfall/hazard/wind.py`).

An initial hypothesis — that CLIMADA's documented `.fillna(0)` behavior for
never-reported RMW might be zeroing Rolly's RMW and collapsing the Holland field toward
zero — was checked directly and **ruled out**: RMW is populated throughout via the JTWC
fallback (5–30 nm), never zero.

## Agency comparison (task 2)

Pulled `usa_*` and `tokyo_*` variables directly from the raw IBTrACS file for Goni
(2020299N11144), converting Tokyo's native 10-minute winds to 1-minute-sustained
equivalent using the same Knapp & Kruk (2010) factor CLIMADA applies internally:
`(v_kn − 23.3) / 0.60`.

- **Position**: USA and Tokyo agree closely throughout (within ~0.1–0.3°) — not a
  meaningful source of disagreement.
- **Intensity**: USA/JTWC runs systematically higher than Tokyo/JMA even after
  rescaling — at peak (2020-10-31 18:00 UTC), USA 170 kn (87.5 m/s) vs Tokyo-rescaled
  161.2 kn (82.9 m/s); at the sample nearest actual landfall (21:00 UTC), USA 150 kn
  (77.2 m/s) vs Tokyo-rescaled 144.5 kn (74.3 m/s). This is the documented JTWC-vs-JMA
  intensity bias for west Pacific typhoons, not new here.
- **Pressure**: a real ~21 hPa disagreement at peak (USA 884 hPa vs Tokyo 905 hPa).
- **RMW**: Tokyo doesn't report it at all — confirmed by checking the raw IBTrACS
  variable list (`tokyo_*` has no `rmw` field), not inferred. USA/JTWC's RMW tightens
  from 45 nm (early, weak) to 5 nm at peak — consistent with a genuinely compact storm.
- **Notable quirk**: Tokyo's rescaled wind and raw pressure are both flat (152.8 kn /
  915 hPa) for five consecutive 3-hourly samples spanning ~24 hours (Oct 30 12:00–Oct 31
  12:00) — consistent with JMA's best-track using a coarse/quantized ceiling
  classification at extreme intensity rather than continuously updating exact values.
  Landfall's currently-used samples are mostly off this plateau (the sample nearest
  landfall, 21:00 UTC, already shows the storm weakening), so this quirk doesn't appear
  to distort the current simulation much, but is worth flagging as a real property of
  the input data.

## Current simulated peak vs observed (task 3) — and a reference-figure correction

Queried the actual synthesized wind field (current default pipeline, unmodified) at
every centroid falling inside Catanduanes' GADM municipality boundaries:
**73.80 m/s, at San Miguel** (Virac 69.8, Baras 67.8 — the three coastal/interior
municipalities closest to the storm's track; **Bato, the reported landfall
municipality, shows only 26.5 m/s** in this simulation — see "what this doesn't
resolve" below, this is flagged, not explained away).

The task's reference figure, "~62 m/s 1-min sustained," needed checking before using it
as a comparison point — comparing wind speeds across agencies without converting
units first is exactly the mistake this phase is designed to avoid, so I checked what
that number actually corresponds to rather than take it at face value:

| Reported figure | Convention | m/s |
|---|---|---|
| JMA/PAGASA "220 km/h" (widely cited as Rolly's landfall intensity) | 10-min sustained, **native, unconverted** | **61.1** |
| Same JMA/PAGASA figure | rescaled to true 1-min-sustained equivalent (Knapp & Kruk factor) | 81.9 |
| JTWC "315 km/h" (also widely cited) | 1-min sustained, native | 87.5 |

**The task's "~62 m/s 1-min sustained" figure is JMA's raw 10-minute-sustained value in
m/s, mislabeled as 1-minute sustained** — it matches the unconverted JMA number almost
exactly (61.1 vs 62). On a genuine apples-to-apples 1-minute-sustained basis (the
convention CLIMADA's `rescale_windspeeds` and the whole model operate in), the correct
comparison figures are **81.9–87.5 m/s**, not 62.

Against the correct reference: **73.8 m/s simulated vs 81.9–87.5 m/s observed — the
current pipeline underestimates peak wind by roughly 8–14 m/s (~10–16%).** That's a
real, if modest, hazard-side gap — not the "near-zero below 30 m/s curve" framing from
Phase 1, and not large enough by itself to explain a 29.6× *damage* error: applying the
Phase 1 RMSF curve to 73.8 m/s vs 87.5 m/s gives mean damage degree 35.1% vs 53.4% at
that single point — a ~1.5× local gap, not 29.6×. This peak-wind shortfall is real but
is not, by itself, the dominant driver of Rolly's remaining damage error.

## Sensitivity tests (task 4)

All measured as max simulated wind over Catanduanes, current curve/config otherwise
unchanged:

| Configuration | Max wind over Catanduanes | vs baseline |
|---|---|---|
| **Baseline** (current default: mixed official_3h + usa-RMW provider, recorded RMW, 150 arcsec grid) | **73.80 m/s** | — |
| (a) Alternate agency: pure USA/JTWC provider | 71.67 m/s | −2.1 m/s |
| (b) RMW: literature/satellite-eye estimate (6.0 nm, Goni's reported 12 nm eye diameter) applied at the 18 peak/compact timesteps, other timesteps left as recorded | 72.93 m/s | −0.9 m/s |
| (c) Grid resolution: 75 arcsec (≈2.3 km), 4× the centroid density | 73.80 m/s | 0.0 m/s (identical) |

**None of the three tested levers improve on baseline; two are slightly worse, one is
exactly identical.** This is reported as a clean negative result, not reworked until
something moved the number: switching to JTWC's track (which runs hotter in its own raw
intensity) doesn't translate into a hotter simulated field once position/pressure shift
along with it; the recorded RMW (5 nm) was already at least as tight as the
literature/satellite estimate (6 nm) so there was no slack to recover there; and the
field is smooth enough (parametric Holland, not a numerical model with sub-grid
turbulence) that 4× the grid density finds the same analytic peak.

## Chosen configuration and E1 (task 5)

**No configuration change is adopted.** None of the tested alternatives are more
physically justified than the current default (which already uses CLIMADA's documented,
standard per-variable agency-fallback behavior and already-recorded RMW), and none
reduce the peak-wind gap either — so there is no error-minimizing-vs-physically-justified
discrepancy to flag here, unusually: the physically-justified choice and the
(non-)improving choice are the same choice, because nothing tested improved anything.

E1 is therefore **unchanged from Phase 1**:

| Storm | Baseline (TDR) | Fix 1: RMSF calibration | Fix 2: hazard-layer (Phase 2) | NDRRMC actual |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Haiyan (2013) | $49.3M (18.6× under) | $775.6M (1.18× under) | *no change adopted* | ~$917M |
| Rolly (2020) | $0.40M (575× under) | $7.86M (29.6× under) | *no change adopted* | ~$233M |
| Odette (2021) — held out | $184.1M (2.5–5.0× under) | $3,532.1M (3.9–7.7× over) | *no change adopted* | $459M–$915M |

Moot in the sense that no configuration changed, so Haiyan's and Odette's numbers above

## What this doesn't resolve — Rolly's error is still ~29.6× under, mostly unexplained

This phase narrows but does not close the gap. What's now known:
- The peak-wind shortfall at the single worst point (73.8 vs 81.9–87.5 m/s) is real but
  modest, and provably too small on its own to explain a 29.6× damage error.
- Track agency, RMW, and grid resolution — the three most obvious hazard-layer levers —
  were each tested and none move the needle.
- A striking, unexplained artifact: **Bato, the actual reported landfall municipality,
  shows only 26.5 m/s in the current simulation** — far below neighboring San Miguel
  (73.8) and Virac (69.8), which are a similar distance from the coast. This could be a
  genuine feature of a very compact storm's track passing closer to one municipality's
  centroid than another, or could indicate the track's exact landfall position is
  slightly off from where Bato's GADM boundary actually sits. Not investigated further
  here — flagged as a concrete, checkable anomaly rather than smoothed over.
- The most likely remaining driver, based on process of elimination rather than direct
  evidence, is the **spatial extent** of high-wind exposure (how much exposed value
  falls in the higher-damage wind range across the whole ROI, not just the single peak
  point) or an **exposure-layer** issue (LitPop's asset distribution for Bicol/
  Catanduanes) — both out of this phase's scope (exposure work is Phase 3).
