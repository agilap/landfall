# Landfall v1.1, Phase 4 result — the waterfall, the flood-share question, and Odette's verdict

## The waterfall (task 1)

| Storm | Baseline (v1, TDR curve) | +PHL curve (Phase 1: RMSF) | +hazard config (Phase 2) | +Bicol exposure (Phase 3) | NDRRMC actual |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Haiyan (2013) | $49.3M — **18.6× under** | $775.6M — **1.18× under** | no change | $775.6M — **1.18× under** | ~$917M |
| Rolly (2020) | $0.40M — **575× under** | $7.86M — **29.6× under** | no change | $91.6M — **2.54× under** | ~$233M |
| Odette (2021) — held out | $184.1M — **2.5–5.0× under** | $3,532.1M — **3.9–7.7× over** | no change | $3,532.1M — **3.9–7.7× over** | $459M–$915M |

Direction matters as much as magnitude here: Haiyan and Rolly moved from under- to
much-closer-to-actual (still under, not overshot); Odette flipped from
under-estimating to over-estimating, and stayed there once flipped — Phases 2 and 3
didn't touch it (zero ROI overlap with the hazard/exposure changes), so its final state
is entirely a Phase 1 (curve) effect.

**Reproducibility**: each storm's historical-baseline `ScenarioConfig` hash is
identical across all four columns —

- Haiyan: `289d30ecbb27bc03`
- Rolly: `d8dc995da27f883e`
- Odette: `76c5b86bf37d2429`

— because the hash is computed from the *scenario* (storm + perturbation), not the
physics configuration (curve/hazard/exposure), by design (`ScenarioConfig.scenario_hash()`
in `landfall/scenario.py`). This means only the **rightmost column** is reproducible by
running today's code (`python -c "from landfall.impact.engine import run_baseline;
run_baseline('rolly')"` after clearing `data/cache/scenarios/`) — the scenario cache
was cleared and regenerated at each phase, so the Baseline/Phase-1/Phase-2 numbers are
historical values recorded and verified in each phase's own commit and result doc
(`docs/v1.1-phase1-result.md`, `docs/v1.1-phase2-result.md`), reproducible by checking
out that commit, not by running the current tree. Stated plainly rather than implying
false reproducibility for cells that aren't reproducible from `main` today.

## The flood/rain-attributable share (task 2) — the sitreps don't support a clean split

Queried the Rolly sitrep corpus (`sitrep_12.pdf`, NDRRMC, as of 11 November 2020) for
its two relevant categorical damage tables:

- **TAB H, "Damage to Infrastructure"** (p.30): grand total ₱12,867,014,693.78
  nationwide, of which **Region V (Bicol) alone: ₱12,226,469,893.60** — almost the
  entire national infrastructure damage total for this storm sits in Bicol.
- **TAB I, "Cost of Damage to Agriculture"** (p.37, sourced explicitly to the
  Department of Agriculture): grand total ₱5,008,430,180.00 nationwide, **Region V:
  ₱3,581,704,608.00**.

**These tables categorize damage by economic sector (agriculture, infrastructure,
housing), not by physical cause (wind, flood, storm surge).** A rice paddy destroyed by
wind-driven lodging and one destroyed by flood inundation appear as the same "RICE"
line item with no distinguishing field; a building with its roof torn off by wind and
one flooded by storm surge both show as "Totally damaged" with a peso amount and no
cause code. The free-text "DESCRIPTION OF DAMAGE" column occasionally names a cause
explicitly — e.g. Bombon, Camarines Sur's water facility: *"Flooded office building,
damaged motor controls and variable frequency drive... submerged water meters"*
(p.35) — but these mentions are sparse and non-systematic, not a basis for a
percentage.

One suggestive, sitrep-grounded (not invented) data point: the sitrep's national
infrastructure table *does* carry an explicit **"FLOOD CONTROL" cost category** for
MIMAROPA region (₱2,514,150,000.00, p.31) — proving NDRRMC's own reporting *can* and
does break out flood-specific damage when material enough to warrant a line item.
**Region V's own infrastructure table has no such flood-specific line at all.** That's
an absence, not a stated percentage, and shouldn't be over-read — but it's weak
evidence that NDRRMC itself didn't consider Bicol's damage flood-dominated enough to
warrant separate accounting, for what that's worth.

**Conclusion, stated as the task allows for**: no defensible numeric flood/rain-share
range can be extracted from this corpus. Reporting "0–X%" would imply more precision
than the source data supports. The honest answer is that this specific question — how
much of Rolly's *recorded* damage is flood/rain-attributable — is not answerable from
NDRRMC's sector-based sitreps; it would need hazard-attributed reporting (e.g. a
post-event forensic damage survey) that doesn't exist in this corpus.

**A separate, unplanned finding surfaced by this investigation**: this same sitrep's
Region V infra + agri total (₱12,226,469,893.60 + ₱3,581,704,608.00 = ₱15,808,174,501.60,
≈ $318.6M at the 2020 average rate) is **higher than the ~$233M ("PHP 11.3B infra+agri")
figure used as Rolly's "NDRRMC actual" in every E1 table so far** (`docs/phase2-result.md`).
Not reconciled here — task 2 asked for a flood-share estimate, not a re-derivation of
E1's reference figure, and changing that number is a bigger decision than this phase's
scope. Flagged for your attention: it's possible the original $233M was drawn from an
earlier, less-complete sitrep number (assessments typically climb as more reports come
in), or a narrower scope than "all of Region V" — worth resolving before treating any
of this waterfall's error factors as final.

## Odette's final verdict (task 4) — the credibility check

Odette started in-range: **2.5–5.0× under**, the only storm sitting inside the PRD's
own stated 2–5× "typical TC-model error" expectation, at v1 baseline. It now sits at
**3.9–7.7× over** — a full reversal, not a drift. This happened entirely in Phase 1
(the RMSF curve swap); Phases 2 and 3 had zero geometric overlap with Odette's ROI and
changed nothing about it.

This is the honest headline risk of the whole v1.1 effort: **the single fix that did
the most good (closing Haiyan's 18.6× gap almost entirely) is the same fix that broke
the one storm that was previously fine.** A single national WP2 curve, calibrated on
aggregate historical damage across many events, cannot simultaneously fit a narrow,
concentrated storm (Rolly, tiny RMW) and a broad one (Odette — Phase 5's own
municipality analysis showed its damage spread across Metro Cebu at a scale Haiyan's
didn't) without one of them landing off. Which one lands off changed between TDR and
RMSF; it didn't stop happening.

This doesn't mean RMSF was the wrong call — Rolly and Haiyan's improvement is real and
large, and RMSF is still the metric the source paper designed specifically to avoid
TDR's large-event-domination bias (Phase 1). But it does mean **this waterfall should
not be read as "the model is now well-calibrated"** — it's better calibrated for two of
three storms, at the cost of the third, and that trade should stay visible every time
this table is shown, not softened into an aggregate "improved" claim.

## What this phase doesn't resolve

- Odette's overestimation is unexplained, not just unfixed — Phase 1 flagged it,
  Phase 4 confirms it's still there and names it as the whole effort's biggest open
  risk, but no cause has been isolated (concentrated-vs-spread damage profile is a
  hypothesis from Phase 1, not confirmed).
- The $233M-vs-$318.6M Rolly reference-figure discrepancy (above) is unresolved.
- Whether a single national impact function can ever fit all three storms
  simultaneously, or whether storm-specific/exposure-density-dependent calibration is
  needed, is an open question this whole v1.1 effort surfaces but doesn't answer.
