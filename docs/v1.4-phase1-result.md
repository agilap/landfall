# Landfall v1.4, Phase 1 result — the two-eye hazard temporal-resolution fix

## Why this phase exists

IBTrACS best-track data is 3-hourly. `landfall/hazard/wind.py` called
`TropCyclone.from_tracks()` on that raw track, and CLIMADA's `from_tracks` computes **one**
wind snapshot per native track point and takes the per-cell maximum across them — with **no
interpolation between points**. At a storm's forward speed the eye moves tens of kilometres
per 3-hourly step (Rolly's median step is 46 km, up to 98 km) while the compact eyewall's
radius of maximum wind (RMW) is as small as ~9–20 km. When the eye's step exceeds roughly
the eyewall footprint's width, consecutive eyewall footprints don't overlap, and the
simulated peak-wind swath breaks into **discrete high-wind rings with low-wind gaps between
them** — an artifact of the sampling cadence, not physics. The gap suppresses per-cell max
wind along the track, which flows straight through the impact function into understated
damage.

The fix is to densify the track to a fine even timestep *before* `from_tracks`, using
CLIMADA's own `TCTracks.equal_timestep()`. No new meteorology, no custom interpolation of
the wind field itself — just sampling the same Holland (1980) field at intermediate eye
positions CLIMADA would otherwise skip.

## Choosing the timestep empirically (coarsest that closes the gap)

A throwaway sweep computed the wind field for the two worst-case (compact-core) storms,
**Rolly** and **Mangkhut**, at `time_step_h ∈ {native 3h, 1.0, 0.5, 0.25}`, and measured
gap closure two ways: the count of high-wind grid cells (does the swath fill in?) and the
number of connected components of the high-wind region (do the discrete rings merge into one
band?). "Eye spacing" below is the median forward step at that cadence.

**Rolly** (forward speed 5.1 / 15.5 / 32.6 km/h min/median/max; eyewall RMW 9–56 km):

| Step | Eye spacing (med) | Peak m/s | ≥33 m/s cells / components | ≥60 m/s cells / components |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| native 3h | 46.5 km | 82.3 | 349 / **4** | 49 / **4** |
| 1.0 h | 15.5 km | 82.3 | 743 / 2 | — |
| **0.5 h** | **7.8 km** | **82.4** | **812 / 2** | **233 / 2** |
| 0.25 h | 3.9 km | 82.4 | 831 / 1 | — |

At native cadence Rolly's hurricane-force swath is literally **four disconnected rings**
(sizes 125/103/78/43 cells at ≥33 m/s; four separate eyewall blobs at ≥60 m/s) — the
textbook two-eye pathology. Resampling merges them: by 0.5 h the swath is one dominant
connected body (811 of 812 cells at ≥33 m/s, with a single residual 1-cell straggler that
only disappears at 0.25 h) and the ≥60 m/s eyewall has gone from four comparable rings to
one dominant band. The high-wind cell count converges as native→1.0→0.5→0.25 =
349→743→**812**→831: the **+9%** jump from 1.0→0.5 shows 1.0 h still under-fills the swath
on Rolly's fastest segment (at 32.6 km/h, 1.0 h leaves 32.6 km between eyes — wider than the
compact eyewall), while the **+2%** from 0.5→0.25 is convergence.

**Mangkhut** (forward speed 15.9 / 28.7 / 44.4 km/h; eyewall RMW 9–65 km):

| Step | Eye spacing (med) | Peak m/s | ≥33 m/s cells / components | ≥60 m/s cells / components |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| native 3h | 86.1 km | 73.7 | 4040 / 1 | 285 / **3** |
| 1.0 h | 28.7 km | 73.7 | 4255 / 1 | — |
| **0.5 h** | **14.3 km** | **73.7** | **4269 / 1** | **629 / 1** |
| 0.25 h | 7.2 km | 73.8 | 4271 / 1 | — |

Mangkhut is a large, fast storm whose *outer* (≥33 m/s) swath happens to stay topologically
connected even at native cadence, so the pathology is invisible at that threshold. It shows
plainly at the **eyewall** (≥60 m/s): native cadence gives **three disconnected eyewall
fragments** (285 cells); 0.5 h merges them into **one continuous band** (629 cells, more than
double the eyewall area). The ≥33 m/s cell count converges 4040→4255→**4269**→4271 — already
flat by 0.5 h.

**Chosen `WIND_TIMESTEP_H = 0.5`.** It is the coarsest step that closes the gap even on the
fastest, most compact segment: at Rolly's peak forward speed (32.6 km/h) 0.5 h gives ~16 km
eye spacing, below ~2× the compact eyewall radius, and the damage-relevant per-cell max
field has converged (peak stable to 0.1 m/s; hi-wind cell count within ~2% of 0.25 h for both
storms). 1.0 h leaves a visible under-fill (Rolly +9% more cells at 0.5 h, still 2 eyewall
components); 0.25 h doubles the compute for a ~2% change. The timestep is folded into the
impact cache key (`engine._cache_key`) so this methodology change invalidates the 28 stale
pre-v1.4 caches rather than serving them (see "Cache invalidation" below).

## E1 waterfall — before → after, point estimate and EDR interquartile range

The point estimate is the EDR `q=0.5` median; the real validation target (per v1.2) is
whether the EDR interquartile range (`q0.75`–`q0.25` = low–high) brackets each storm's
recorded actual. All figures are the historical (zero-perturbation) baselines from
`run_baseline()`, recomputed under the new cache key.

| Storm | Before (point) | **After (point)** | After EDR range (low–high) | Recorded actual | Error factor (after point) | Range brackets? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Haiyan (2013) | $695.4M | **$917.6M** | $67.3M – $3.66B | ~$917M | **1.00× (≈ exact)** | **✓** |
| Rolly (2020) | $81.4M | **$190.4M** | $12.4M – $1.19B | ~$233M | **1.22× under** | **✓** |
| Odette (2021) | $3.10B | **$5.31B** | $335M – $30.1B | $459M – $915M | **5.8–11.6× over** | **✓** |
| Mangkhut (2018) | $126.0M | **$197.3M** | $12.1M – $1.34B | $639.8M (see note) | **3.24× under** | **✓** |

**Every storm's damage rose, none fell** — the expected direction for a gap-filling fix
(interpolation raises the per-cell max, never lowers it). This is the sanity check the
change had to pass, and it did for all four. Affected-population also rose slightly
(Haiyan 9.33M→9.36M, Rolly 2.93M→3.34M, Odette 10.54M→10.56M, Mangkhut 0.74M→0.77M),
consistent with a modestly wider nonzero-wind footprint.

**The EDR interquartile range still brackets all four storms' actuals**, including Mangkhut
(a genuine out-of-sample check — the range calibration was never fit to it) and Odette (see
below). The v1.2 finding — that the already-published per-event EDR quantile spread brackets
these storms simultaneously — survives the hazard fix.

### Per-storm reading

- **Haiyan** improved from 1.32× under to essentially exact ($917.6M vs ~$917M). Read this
  as a coincidentally tight point landing, not a precision claim — the honest uncertainty is
  the range ($67M–$3.66B), which is wide.
- **Rolly** improved from 2.86× under to 1.22× under — the storm the fix was expected to help
  most (its compact core suffered the worst two-eye gap), and it did.
- **Mangkhut** improved from 5.08× under to 3.24× under, staying comfortably inside its
  range.

### The Odette finding — reported plainly, not tuned (approved policy)

**Odette's point estimate overshoots the recorded actual by ~5.8–11.6× ($5.31B vs the
$459M–$915M NDRRMC band), and the overshoot got *worse* with this fix, not better ($3.10B →
$5.31B).** This is the expected but uncomfortable consequence of a gap-filling fix applied
uniformly: it raises every storm's damage, and Odette was already the storm the single
national EDR curve overshoots (documented since v1.1 Phase 5 / v1.2 Phase 1). Filling its
wind gaps pushes an already-high number higher.

Per the approved honesty policy, this is documented, not hidden, and **no per-storm fudge
factor was added** to pull Odette down — doing so would be exactly the custom-tuning the
project's non-goals forbid. What keeps this a coherent result rather than a failure: the
**EDR range still brackets Odette's actual** ($335M low < $459M actual-low; $30.1B high >
$915M actual-high). The point estimate is wrong for Odette in the same direction and for the
same reason it was already wrong; the fix didn't introduce a new pathology, it amplified a
known one. The range — not the point — remains the honest deliverable for Odette.

## Cache invalidation (the critical correctness step)

`_cache_key()` previously folded scenario + calibration + ROI but **not** the wind timestep.
Changing the wind computation without touching the key would have left the 28 existing caches
holding stale native-cadence damage under unchanged keys — the exact methodology-drift bug
`_cache_key`'s own docstring warns about. `"wind_timestep_h": WIND_TIMESTEP_H` was added to
the fingerprint, so every baseline got a **new** cache key and the old files went
unreferenced by `run()`.

One consequence surfaced that the original plan mis-anticipated. The plan said old caches
could be left in place as "harmless unreferenced" files. That is true for `run()` (keyed by
`_cache_key` = filename) but **not** for the viz exporter: `export_viz.find_cache_by_scenario_hash`
looks caches up by their embedded user-facing `scenario_hash` (which does *not* depend on
calibration or timestep), so an old and a new baseline cache share the same `scenario_hash`
and the exporter's ambiguity guard correctly **refused to export** ("matched 2 cache files").
The fix that restores the exporter's one-cache-per-`scenario_hash` invariant is the same one
the project has always used on a methodology change (v1.1: "Scenario cache cleared"):
the 28 stale pre-v1.4 caches (identifiable by the absent `wind_timestep_h` field) were
deleted. They are regenerable and gitignored; the 24 counterfactuals among them are
recomputed on demand (E2 does so under the new key).

## Determinism

`run_baseline('rolly', use_cache=False)` run twice produced **byte-identical** cache JSON
(sha256 match). The pipeline remains deterministic under the fix.

## What this phase changed

- `src/landfall/hazard/wind.py`: `WIND_TIMESTEP_H = 0.5`; `wind_field` now deep-copies the
  track and calls `equal_timestep(0.5)` before `from_tracks`. The deep copy is load-bearing:
  `equal_timestep` mutates in place and `export_viz` writes `track.json` from the same tracks
  object right after calling `wind_field`, so densifying in place would have silently turned
  the exported "raw observed IBTrACS track" into a sub-hourly interpolation. Verified: exported
  `track.json` point counts are unchanged (Haiyan 57, Odette 59, Rolly 61, Mangkhut 85 — the
  raw track), while the wind grids changed.
- `src/landfall/impact/engine.py`: `wind_timestep_h` added to both `_cache_key`'s fingerprint
  and the `run()` result dict.
- `src/landfall/export_viz.py`: `wind_timestep_h` copied into each bundle's `meta.json` for
  provenance.
- `tests/test_export_viz.py`: `_WIND_PEAK_PINS` re-pinned to the new (higher, relocated)
  peaks, orientation re-verified against each track's ROI-internal peak-intensity segment
  before pinning (peak cell within ~1–1.5× RMW of the track, on its path, no row flip).

## What this phase doesn't resolve

- **Odette's point-estimate overshoot** (above) — a pre-existing single-curve limitation the
  fix amplifies, left documented and un-tuned per policy.
- The **absolute value** of the peak-wind rise is only as good as the Holland field and the
  EDR curve; densification makes the sampling faithful to the model, it doesn't make the
  model itself more physically correct.

## What to verify manually before trusting this

- **Mangkhut's NDRRMC actual figure.** The waterfall uses **$639.8M**, the PH damage total
  established in `docs/v1.3-phase1-result.md` (SitRep No. 50, ₱33.69B combined
  Infrastructure + Agriculture at the 2018 WB rate). The v1.4 task brief instead described
  Mangkhut as having "no PH actual (mostly struck China)" — that is inconsistent with the
  repo's own v1.3 record, and this doc follows the documented v1.3 figure rather than the
  brief. Mangkhut *did* go on to cause larger damage in Guangdong/Hong Kong after its Cagayan
  landfall, but its Philippine damage is real and documented; if a reviewer wants the
  waterfall to instead treat Mangkhut as PH-actual-unavailable, that is a one-line change,
  but the bracketing verdict (range contains $639.8M) would simply become "n/a".
- The timestep sweep numbers above are reproducible via the same method the throwaway
  `scratch_timestep_sweep.py` used (deleted after this phase) — a 33 m/s and 60 m/s
  connected-component count at each cadence.
- Whether 0.5 h is the right cost/accuracy trade for the *counterfactual* grid too (the sweep
  was on historical baselines only); the same constant governs both.
